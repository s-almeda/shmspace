background & related work
Literature on CST use has largely focused on single-context tool affordances, domain-specific needs, and further enabling novices and experts rather than behaviors which exist outside of or counter to developer intentions [13, 14, 34]. In our study, we explore the term “misuse” as a behavior of creative practitioners towards creativity support tools. We position our findings in connection to literature studying artists and their use of CSTs qualitatively, and research on the design and practice of appropriation, especially through hacker, DIY, and sustainability communities.
This work aligns with creativity support literature that looks beyond the technology itself to investigate the external contexts that influence users. Previous studies identify how contextual factors [8] and practitioner values [23, 29], while external to tool interfaces, contribute to a better understanding of creative practice and tool use. Beyond needs, researchers emphasize the importance of value sensitive design which considers human values alongside technical considerations.[15] Chung et al. construct a design space from prior CST literature that considers how user groups and roles intersect with aspects of tool design [6], and further investigate how artists fit CSTs into their existing support networks [6].
Through studying expert creative practitioners’ process, previous work argues for CST design that amplifies existing strategies [27]. We build on work investigating the intersection of power and CSTs [24] to consider how power dynamics create perceptions of “correct” tool use, and subsequently misuse, in creative practices. Roedl et al. discuss the transition from studying the “user” to the “maker” as an agent capable of more than simple “use” [36]. Our study extends existing research that considers creative practitioners’ perspectives, values, and processes in HCI to study how values and contexts intersect with tool use and misuse.
This work imagines the term “misuse” with a distinct set of connotations, drawing from Ahmed’s study of the word “use” to include notions of utility, institutionalism, and relation: “Use is a relation as well as an activity that often points beyond something. . . to use something points to what something is for” [1]. Indeed, one of the goals of this paper is to destabilize “misuse” to consider a diversity of artist interpretations of the concept. One interpretation of misuse familiar to HCI is appropriation. Appropriation is just one of the many facets of misuse suggested by our findings; we use this literature to understand this notion of political movement in the context of creative practice, especially as artists engage in misuse to resist authority. Eglash defines appropriation as the adaptation or reinvention of products [11]. Through this lens, appropriation is not only an opportunity for technical innovation but also a movement away from institutionalization, signaling a democratization of design. Previous work has examined “appropriation” in many domains, often from the angle of design invitations, and from the designer perspective. Researchers have explored how colleagues cultivate appropriation methods with limited tools in collaborative workplaces [32, 43]. An interpersonal understanding of appropriation considers communication as a means to share use cases [21] and as the purpose of appropriation itself [37]. Researchers have proposed ways to design for appropriation [3, 10, 26], an implicit oxymoron that Dix addresses: that we might allow, not expect, unexpected use [9].
In this work, we examine appropriation from the practitioner perspective and seek to understand how, why, and in what contexts they misuse tools, resisting the angle of proposing solutions for designers.
Studies of appropriation that center creative practitioners themselves include those that identify the “everyday creativity” present when households [42]or communities adapt objects in unexpected ways. Salovaara et al. explore appropriation as a fact of our daily lives as simple as using a phone as a mirror [38]. Participants in LARPs [39] and steampunk communities [2] introduce unexpected technologies, using appropriation as a vector for creative exploration and personal empowerment. In contrast, this work centers creative practitioners’ own perceptions regarding their “misuse” of authoring tools. Hacking and DIY are often discussed as forms of appropriation which focus on transformative or additive adaptation: opportunistic practices [19]defined not only by their methods but also their communities and values [5]. Galloway suggests these practices are a way to “reclaim authorship and ownership of technologies and the social and cultural worlds in which we live” [16]. We draw inspiration from this literature to identify and understand behaviors along similar veins of reclamation and opportunity. Central to concepts of “appropriation” and “hacking” is normativity: a notion of “standard” to stand against. Pre- vious work has explored methods such as disassembly 25], destruction 12], breakdown [20], ambiguity 17], and counterfunctionality [31]to reveal the opportunities for empowerment and moments of playfulness possible when engaging with alternatives to standard practices. Costanza-Chock’s work on design justice highlights the political dimension of misuse as a form of empowerment and possibility for self- and collective governance, as distinct from technosolutionism [7]. In positioning our paper within the realms of appropriation and hacking, we consider not only the unorthodox techniques and methods of creative practitioners, but also the contexts in which artists misuse, as well as the implications of defying norms.
