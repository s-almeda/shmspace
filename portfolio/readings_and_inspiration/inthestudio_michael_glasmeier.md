in response to Michael Glasmeier's "In the Studio"

The death of painting as addressed in this passage is connected to the proliferation of photography, film, and new media as well as the loss of interest in abstract art. Claiming that painting has died is described as inevitable considering how much it inherently experiments with pushing or transcending its own boundaries. This relates back to earlier in the passage where Glasmeier claims that Rembrandt's "An Artist in His Studio" is proof of several points about painting itself, inluding that "realism has always been on the edge of abstraction" and that "it is necessary and sensible for painters to be aware of the concepts of their time, something we could refer to today as visual or media theory," even saying that in any work it "must be present." If abstraction in painting has been done away with or rejected, "mutated into the props of a poster culture for teenage girls' bedrooms," and pushing the boundaries of and experimenting with realism is much more suited to photography, film, and new media, what use is there to paint anymore? 

In my opinion, Glasmeier is trying to convey that **painting's recourse post "death" is that, in this day and age, choosing to make a piece of art as a painting inherently forces the work to be about, in some capacity, the act of painting and the history of painting itself.**  Glasmeier quotes Beckett as writing, "What renews painting is first of all that there are always more things to paint…" and in an age of endless photographic images of literally anything, the choices of things to paint is infinite in a new way and **the choice to paint is meaningful in a new way.** 

A modern painter is, just as Rembrandt was, aware of the visual and media concepts of the time and (unless you are a "naive painter") **this acknowledgement is present in every painting inherently because of its existence as a painting**, its implication of the "reality of mental space" that photography does not necessarily portray in the same way.
