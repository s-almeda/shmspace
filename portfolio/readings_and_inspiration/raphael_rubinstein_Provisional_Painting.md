Response to *Provisional Painting* 

Many of the works discussed in this reading, Provisional Painting by Raphael Rubinstein, have a profound “lack of finish,” a “rejection of the idea of a finished, durable work,” qualities of an “unfinished” piece, which leads a viewer and reader to ask what makes these pieces feel unfinished. Is it the speed or carelessness portrayed in the marks? If so, that would suggest that a finished painting requires someone to spend a certain amount of time or effort on it before it is finished, or to portray a certain amount of skill. For instance, visual indications that the painter planned the piece, returned to certain marks, perfected them, etc. In Rubinstein’s words, **“many other painters seek to convey their artistic ambitions through signs of intensive labor, grand scale, daunting complexity or serious themes.”**

Artists like De Keyser, Oehlen, Wool, Heilmann, etc. as discussed in the reading are rejecting these methods of gauging the value of a work in a manner that feels somewhat cynical/critical, like **a disposal of formality and sentimentality in favor of immediacy and a kind of virtuous laziness.** Rubinstein writes of Oehlen’s work, “...many of them look as if someone had inadvertently spilled paint onto a poster and, in the attempt to clean it off, had only made matters worse.”

This sort of *irreverent attitude towards painting* is something I can really get behind, and aspire for. Leaving the canvas riddled with “mistakes” is bold. It asks the viewer, why should I have to work any harder? To fulfill your arbitrary idea of what a good painter is like? Indeed I think this concept of ego controls a lot of people and their painting style, myself included — the thought of “I can’t leave that in! They’ll think I’m lazy! A real painter would fix that mistake, and do it over until it’s perfect!” often paralyzes me in the same area of a painting until I feel satisfied that the painting won’t “embarrass” me. I aspire for Kimber Smith’s “apparent nonchalance” and “freedom from anxiety” and I agree with Rubinstein that “provisionality” isn’t a rejection of painting as a medium but an opportunity to expand its definition and find new meaning in the act of painting without being wrapped up in its seemingly inherent grandeur.
